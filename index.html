A Brief Summary of Colorado 14ers

Colorado's Highest Peaks

Long's Peak (Elev: 14,256')

1/3

The lone 14er in Colorado's Rocky Mountain National Park, Long's Peak is one of the state's most popular hiking spots. After it's first ascent in 1868 by John Wesley Powell, 15,000 hikers and climbers attempt to summit it each year. About half will see the views from the top. Partly due to its traffic and partly due to its difficulty it has earned the dubious reputation of "Colorado's Deadliest Mountain".

By far the most popular route to climb this beast is via the "Keyhole Route". This route brings it's victims up a deceptively easy 7.4 mile hike to the Boulderfields, a barren wasteland of boulders sitting just below the summit. This is a popular spot for overnight campers. After the Boulderfields climbers will traverse through the route's iconic keyhole and along narrow exposed routes, ending in a 300 foot Class 3 scramble to the summit.

This hike is not for the faint of heart. Hikers die summiting Long's Peak each year. If you plan to attempt it, be prepared for a lot of exposure and technical hiking, potentially requiring crampons and some serious climbing skills.

Mt. Democrat (Elev: 14,152')

1/3

Sitting in the Mosquito Range of the Rocky Mountains, Mt. Democrat is a common beginner's peak for those just starting their journey into Colorado high alpine climbing. Sitting just west of Breckenridge near the town of Alma, the peak provides easy access for climbers across the state.

Many climbers will choose to tackle this peak via the Kite Lake Trailhead. Be sure to get started early as the parking lot will fill up quick, especially on holiday weekends. Those looking for an extra challenge can tackle the DeCaLiBro, a route which summits Mt. Democrat, Mt. Cameron, Mt. Lincoln, and Mt. Bross all in a single trip.

While this mountain itself is relatively easy, it is still a 14er. Hikers should be prepared for a low oxygen environment and as always, be wary of rapidly changing weather conditions on the mountain.

Capitol Peak (Elev: 14,137')

1/3

Towering over the Maroon Bells-Snowmass Wilderness, Capitol Peak is a daunting task for even the most daring of high-alpine enthusiasts. With an elevation gain of over 6,000' over Snowmass Village, hikers are faced with a choice of a daunting scramble over the severe exposure or a highly technical climb. Although the views from the peak are incredible, approach this one with caution.

There are two possible routes to summit this monster. The first of which involves a hike around K2, which many hikers will mistake for a false summit. After scrambling it's rocky face, hikers will scramble over the knife's edge, a 150 foot long exposed segment with 2000' drop-offs on either side. The other option is a Class 4 scramble leading to a grade 5.9 rope climb with significant falling rock hazard.

Capitol Peak is widely known as one of Colorado's most dangerous hikes. Forgetting the severe exposure for a second, the route also exposes its victims to a significant falling rock hazard. Helmets are a must on this route. Do NOT take shortcuts on this route. Hikers die every year trying to get down the mountain in record time. Follow the route and know what you're getting into before you go.
